## 2. Entry Strategy — How to Get the First Meeting

### 2.1 The Cold Outreach That Works

The single biggest mistake in selling to theatre organisations: sending a generic "we have a SaaS product that could help you" email. Theatre professionals receive these constantly. The email that gets a reply is one that demonstrates you understand their specific situation, their specific show, and the specific economic opportunity.

**Target:** Martin Crosier, Managing Director, LW Tickets
**Secondary:** Matt Simkin, Head of Commercial, LW Theatres
**Channel:** LinkedIn message first (lower friction than cold email), followed by email to reception@lwtheatres.co.uk if no reply in 5 business days.

---

**LinkedIn message template (Martin Crosier):**

> Hi Martin,
>
> I'm Joe Katz — I'm building a seat upgrade platform specifically for West End theatre, and I think Totoro is the perfect first show to pilot it with.
>
> The core idea: patrons who bought restricted-view or side stalls seats 6 months ago get a T-7 email inviting them to bid for an upgrade into central stalls. We run an optimisation solver at T-3, capture payment, and the box office swaps the seats. No risk to LW Theatres — pure revenue share, zero upfront.
>
> For a typical mid-week Totoro performance, conservative modelling suggests ~20 confirmed upgrades averaging £38 each = ~£470 net to LW Theatres per show, ~£3,700/week across the run.
>
> Happy to walk through the economics in more detail — 30 minutes, whenever suits you.
>
> Joe

**Why this works:**
- Named the show specifically (not "your venues")
- Led with the money (not the technology)
- Given a concrete, believable number
- Asked for 30 minutes, not a commitment

## 3. The Pitch Narrative

### 3.1 The Core Story (Two-Minute Version)

*This is the story you tell in the first two minutes of any meeting. It must work without slides.*

---

> "My Neighbour Totoro has one of the most loyal audiences of any current West End production. A lot of those patrons bought their tickets a year ago and ended up in restricted-view stalls or side circle — not because they wanted to sit there, but because that's what was available when they booked.
>
> Right now, those patrons are sitting next to someone who bought a £25 day seat that morning and is sitting in exactly the same section, possibly with a better angle.
>
> We think that's a missed opportunity — for the patrons and for you.
>
> The Shakeup sends those advance-booking patrons an email seven days before their performance, inviting them to bid for an upgrade into central stalls or front circle. They name their price — anything above a floor you set — and we run an optimisation process three days out that assigns the best matches. Successful bidders pay and get a new ticket. Unsuccessful bidders hear back immediately and keep their original seat at no cost.
>
> For you: no upfront cost, no marketing spend, no additional technology to manage. We handle everything. You take 75% of every successful upgrade bid. For a show of Totoro's scale, mid-week, we're estimating £400–700 net to you per performance.
>
> We'd like Totoro to be the first show we run this on."

---

### 3.3 The RSC / Production Narrative (Different Meeting, Different Story)

The RSC and the Totoro production team care about different things to LW Theatres. Their conversation is about audience experience, brand integrity, and patron satisfaction — not primarily P&L.

**The production pitch:**

> "Your audience is one of the most passionate in West End theatre. Many of them planned this trip for months, bought tickets early, and ended up in seats they feel slightly short-changed by.
>
> We give them an opportunity to be moved — at their own choice, their own price, with no risk if it doesn't happen. The patrons who upgrade will have a materially better experience of the show. The patrons who don't win still feel valued because they were offered the chance.
>
> The email goes out under your branding, from your domain. To your audience, it looks like a loyalty initiative from the Totoro production — not a third-party service. We're invisible to your patrons unless they look closely.
>
> We have zero ability to send any communication to your patrons without your explicit approval of the copy, the timing, and the branding. Every email is subject to your sign-off before it goes."

**The Studio Ghibli brand angle:**
The production holds a Ghibli licence. The upgrade email must not use Studio Ghibli imagery or characters without explicit approval from the Nippon TV/Ghibli side. This should be raised directly and resolved in the second meeting — either The Shakeup's email uses only the "My Neighbour Totoro" wordmark and venue branding (safest), or Nippon TV/RSC pre-approves a limited use of branded assets for the upgrade email (premium experience, but requires more legal clearance). Propose the conservative option first.

## 4. The Three-Meeting Process

### Meeting 1 — LW Theatres Commercial Team (30–45 mins)

**Goal:** Get agreement in principle that LW Theatres is interested and willing to bring the production team into a conversation.

**What to bring:**
- A one-page "leave-behind" with the financial model (above) and the four-point value proposition
- Your LinkedIn profile showing data engineering / product background (credibility)
- A 5-slide deck maximum (problem → solution → economics → pilot terms → ask)

**Questions to ask (as important as what you say):**
1. "What's your current approach to late-stage inventory in the final week before a show? Is there anything you're doing already with day seats or rush tickets?"
2. "Who else would need to be involved in a decision like this — do you work directly with the Totoro production team on audience initiatives?"
3. "What would a successful pilot look like to you? What numbers would make you want to roll this out to the rest of the LW estate?"

**The ask at the end of Meeting 1:**
*"I'd love to get 30 minutes with you and whoever from the Totoro production team handles the audience relationship. If LW Theatres is open to exploring this, would you be willing to make that introduction?"*

Do NOT ask for a signed anything in Meeting 1. Ask for the introduction.

---

### Meeting 2 — Joint Session: LW Theatres + RSC / Totoro Production (45–60 mins)

**Goal:** Both parties aligned on the model; agree to move to LOI stage.

**Structure:**
- 10 mins: Recap of the product for the production team (they weren't in Meeting 1)
- 15 mins: Walk through the patron journey — the email, the seat map, the bid flow, the outcome emails. Have mockups, even rough ones.
- 10 mins: Address the production team's brand concerns directly
- 10 mins: Walk through the LOI terms (see Section 5)
- 15 mins: Open questions

**The question to answer before this meeting:**
*"What does the Totoro production's standard patron communication protocol look like? Does the RSC approve all patron emails, or does LW Theatres handle that?"* Find this out between Meetings 1 and 2 so you're not caught flat-footed.

**The ask at the end of Meeting 2:**
*"We'd like to send you a draft LOI this week. It's short — two pages. We've designed it to be zero-risk for both of you. The only thing we need from you is a named person from each side who will own the relationship with us during the pilot."*

---

### Meeting 3 — LOI Negotiation (30 mins or async)

**Goal:** Signed LOI from both parties.

Most of this meeting happens in a Word document via tracked changes, not in person. Be responsive: any redline that comes back from their solicitor should be returned within 24 hours. Slow responses kill deals.

## 5. The Pilot LOI Term Sheet

This is the commercial framework you propose. It is intentionally favourable to the venue to reduce friction. You negotiate from here if needed, but try not to.

---

### KEY COMMERCIAL TERMS

**Duration:** 3 months from first eligible performance (approximately 100 performances based on 8/week). Automatically extends month-by-month unless either party gives 2 weeks written notice.

**Revenue split:** 75% to LW Theatres / Totoro production (split between them per their own commercial arrangement — The Shakeup does not need to adjudicate this). 25% to The Shakeup. No minimum guaranteed revenue. No performance fee.

**The Shakeup provides at no cost:**
- Patron bid portal (web application)
- T–7 upgrade offer email (copy subject to venue/production approval)
- Pre-authorisation processing and payment capture (via Stripe)
- Optimisation solver (runs automatically at T–3)
- All patron outcome communications (bid receipt, T–3 result, new ticket PDF)
- Resolution summary email to box office team per performance
- Manager dashboard (real-time bid tracking, post-show analytics)
- Customer support for patron queries related to the upgrade service
- Post-pilot analytics report

**LW Theatres / production provides:**
- Booking data access (daily CSV export, or future API access)
- Approval of email template and branding (within 5 business days of submission)
- Box office cooperation for manual ticket swaps during the pilot
- Named "Upgrade Champion" from the box office team
- Approval to send upgrade offer emails to eligible patrons using the venue/production email domain

**Exclusivity:** During the pilot, LW Theatres agrees not to operate a competing patron seat upgrade auction service for Totoro performances at the Gillian Lynne Theatre. The Shakeup agrees not to approach other shows currently running at the Gillian Lynne Theatre during the pilot without LW Theatres' written consent.

**Data:** All patron personal data remains owned by the venue/production. The Shakeup processes it solely to deliver the upgrade service. A Data Processing Agreement (DPA) compliant with UK GDPR Article 28 is signed concurrently with this LOI. The Shakeup may use anonymised, aggregated outcome data (no personal identifiers) for product improvement and demand model training.

**Pilot success review:** At 6 weeks, The Shakeup provides a mid-pilot review report. At 3 months, both parties review against the KPIs below and decide on continuation/expansion.

**IP:** The Shakeup retains all IP in the platform, solver, and demand models. Venue retains all IP in their patron data and seat maps.

**Liability cap:** The Shakeup's liability to LW Theatres is capped at the total revenue share received from LW Theatres in the 3 months prior to any claim. Neither party is liable for indirect or consequential losses.

**Termination for cause:** Either party may terminate immediately if the other materially breaches this agreement and fails to cure within 10 business days of written notice.

## 6. The Box Office Champion Playbook

This is the operational piece that determines whether the pilot actually works day-to-day. From risk R01: box office friction is the highest-probability failure mode in the MVP phase.

### 6.1 Finding the Champion

In Meeting 2 or 3, ask specifically: *"Who manages the day-to-day box office operations at the Gillian Lynne? We'd love to spend 30 minutes with them before we go live to make sure the process is as simple as possible for them."*

The champion should be the person who: (a) has authority over box office workflows, (b) is respected by the team, and (c) ideally sees this as an interesting new initiative rather than extra admin. Do not accept "just email the general box office inbox" — you need a named person.

### 6.2 The Box Office Onboarding Session (30 mins, in person at the venue)

**Agenda:**
1. Walk through exactly what happens at T–3: "You'll receive an email at approximately [time] on [date] with a table of upgrades to process."
2. Show them the exact format of the resolution email — every column pre-explained.
3. Walk through the Line-Up workflow for a seat swap together, live in their system.
4. Time it: if it takes more than 4 minutes per upgrade, identify the friction point and simplify.
5. Give them a one-page reference card (laminated, if they'd like).
6. Exchange mobile numbers. "If anything is confusing on the first show, text me."

### 6.3 The Resolution Email Format

Every resolution email sent to the box office team must be formatted for minimum processing time. No narrative, no explanation — just the table:

```
Subject: [TOTORO UPGRADES] Thursday 15 October | 7:30pm | 22 upgrades to process

Please process the following seat swaps before show day. 
Contact Joe (07XXX XXXXXX) with any questions.

BOOKING REF  | PATRON EMAIL          | OLD SEAT          | NEW SEAT           | DONE?
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
BK-2847392   | sarah@email.com       | Stalls Side C14   | Stalls Centre F7   | [ ]
BK-2847401   | james@email.com       | Circle Side A3    | Stalls Centre H12  | [ ]
...

All swaps must be completed by 5pm on show day.
New tickets have been emailed to patrons by The Shakeup — patrons should use new ticket for entry.

If any swap cannot be completed, please call Joe immediately: 07XXX XXXXXX
```

### 6.4 Recognising the Champion

At the end of the 3-month pilot:
- Send a personal thank-you letter to the box office champion from The Shakeup (not an email — a physical letter, signed).
- Include their team in the pilot success metrics: "The Gillian Lynne box office team processed 287 upgrades with zero errors in 100 performances."
- If the pilot goes to a full group deal, advocate for a small operational fee for the venue (e.g., £1–2 per manually processed upgrade) as a recognition of box office effort — this converts the team from a cost centre to a mini-profit centre and completely changes their motivation.

## 7. Pilot KPIs & Conversion to Full Partnership

### 7.1 Success KPIs

These are the metrics that determine whether the pilot converts to a long-term partnership. Agree them with LW Theatres before the pilot starts — set expectations in writing.

| KPI | Target | Minimum Threshold |
|-----|--------|------------------|
| Patron satisfaction (post-upgrade survey, 1–5) | ≥ 4.2 / 5 | ≥ 3.8 / 5 |
| Seat conflict incidents (patron at wrong seat) | 0 | 0 |
| Chargeback rate | < 0.2% | < 0.5% |
| Upgrades confirmed per eligible performance | ≥ 18 | ≥ 10 |
| Patron email open rate | ≥ 38% | ≥ 25% |
| Bid placement rate (of openers) | ≥ 18% | ≥ 10% |
| Gross upgrade revenue per performance | ≥ £600 | ≥ £300 |
| Box office processing time per upgrade | ≤ 4 min | ≤ 8 min |
| T–3 solver job success rate | 100% | 100% |

**If minimum thresholds are not met after 6 weeks:** Mid-pilot review triggered. Identify root cause (email copy, seat map config, bid flow friction, participation rate) and implement targeted fix. Do not wait until month 3.

### 7.2 The Conversion Conversation

If KPIs are met, the expansion conversation at month 3 should propose:
1. **Extend Totoro** through to the January 2027 close (already natural given the run)
2. **Add a second LW Theatres show** — ideally a different production at a different LW venue (London Palladium or Theatre Royal Drury Lane) to prove venue-agnostic scalability
3. **Propose a group framework agreement** with LW Theatres covering all 6 venues, replacing show-by-show LOIs with a single master commercial agreement

The financial case for the group deal:
```
6 LW venues × average 6 shows/week × £684 net/show = £24,624/week → £1.28M/year
to LW Theatres from upgrade revenue
```

That number — in the context of a 3-month pilot that cost them nothing — is the conversion argument.

## 8. Parallel Pipeline — Second Venue (Must Run Simultaneously)

Per risk R28, the business cannot afford to be single-show dependent. The second venue LOI should be in progress at the same time as the Totoro pilot launch — not after.

### Target Profile: Spektrix-Powered Independent West End Venue

**Why Spektrix:** Open partner program, free API access, no gatekeeper. The second venue integration can be built concurrently with the Totoro manual MVP.

**Target shows:** A long-running West End production at an independent venue — preferably something with:
- Strong advance booking patterns (audience bought early)
- Known restricted-view inventory (corner stalls, side circle)
- A smaller but passionate fanbase (musical, rather than a play — musicals have repeat attendees)

**Candidate category:** Established musical in mid-to-late run at a Nederlander, Barbican, or similar Spektrix client venue. The Barbican specifically uses Spektrix and runs RSC productions — there's an RSC relationship already established through Totoro that could facilitate an introduction.

**Outreach approach:** More direct than LW Theatres — for an independent venue, a one-meeting LOI is realistic because there's one decision-maker (the producer or venue director) rather than two (LW + RSC).

## 9. Trade Press & PR Strategy

### 9.1 The Stage — Primary Channel

**The Stage** (thestage.co.uk) is the trade publication read by every venue manager, producer, and box office director in the UK. A well-placed article in The Stage is worth ten cold emails.

**Target moments:**
1. **LOI signing announcement** (brief): *"New upgrade platform The Shakeup signs pilot with LW Theatres for My Neighbour Totoro"* — 150 words, embargoed until both parties approve.
2. **Pilot results feature** (3 months later): *"How a seat upgrade algorithm generated £68,000 in incremental revenue for Totoro — and what it means for West End yield management"* — full case study with numbers. This is the inbound generation piece.

**The Stage editorial contact:** Approach via editorial@thestage.co.uk with a news tip. Stage journalists are interested in innovation stories with real numbers.

### 9.2 WhatsOnStage — Consumer PR

Once the product is live and patron-facing, a piece in WhatsOnStage aimed at theatre audiences — *"How to upgrade your theatre seat for less than you think"* — drives patron awareness and participation rates. This is Month 2–3, not Month 1.

### 9.3 LinkedIn — Founder Profile

The founder's LinkedIn should be active in the theatre and arts tech space before the first meeting:
- Post about the problem (the rush ticket / advance booking fairness gap) as a thought leadership piece
- Engage with LW Theatres, RSC, and UK Theatre content
- Build a visible profile as someone who thinks carefully about theatre economics

This matters because the first thing Martin Crosier's team does after receiving your LinkedIn message is click your profile. It should tell a coherent, credible story.
